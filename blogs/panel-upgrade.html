5 Signs Your Electrical Panel Needs an Upgrade

Your panel is the heart of your home’s electrical system. If it’s undersized, outdated, or showing warning signs, upgrading can improve safety, reliability, and your ability to power modern appliances.

1) Breakers trip often (or you’re “living” at the panel)

Occasional trips can happen, but frequent trips are a signal that a circuit is overloaded, a breaker is failing, or there’s a wiring issue. If you’re resetting breakers weekly, the system isn’t keeping up with demand.

Why it matters: Repeated tripping is a safety feature doing its job. The goal is to fix the underlying cause—often by adding capacity, balancing circuits, or upgrading the panel.

2) Lights flicker, dim, or surge when large appliances start

If your lights noticeably dim when the HVAC kicks on, the dryer runs, or you charge an EV, your electrical system may be stretched thin. Sometimes it’s a single circuit problem; other times it’s an overall service capacity issue.

What we check: Load calculations, panel condition, main connections, and whether circuits are properly distributed.

3) The panel feels warm, smells “hot,” or you see discoloration

A warm panel door, hot breakers, a burning smell, or brown marks around breakers are red flags. Heat usually means resistance—loose connections, damaged bus bars, or components that are deteriorating.

Important: If you smell burning from an outlet or panel, turn off power at the main (if safe) and call an electrician immediately.

4) You have an older panel type or outdated electrical setup

Some older panels are known for reliability issues or limited safety features. Even if your panel “still works,” age can mean worn components, brittle insulation, and an increased risk of failure under modern electrical loads.

Modern upgrades include: safer breaker technology, better surge protection options, cleaner labeling, and code-compliant grounding/bonding.

5) You’re adding big electrical loads (EV charger, remodel, hot tub, workshop)

New projects often require more circuits and more total capacity. If your panel is full or your service size is too small, you’ll run into limitations—or end up with a patchwork of temporary fixes.

Best approach: Upgrade once, do it right, and give your home room to grow.

What a panel upgrade typically includes

A proper upgrade isn’t just swapping a box. It usually includes a load calculation, code-compliant grounding and bonding, proper breaker sizing, clean circuit labeling, and a final inspection where required.
